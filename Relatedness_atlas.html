Bateson–Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibility (BDMI) screen. For each inversion candidate, runs six population-genetic tests that flag candidate incompatibility regions: Mendelian segregation distortion, missing karyotype classes, heterozygote excess / deficit, ancestry × genotype interaction, long-range forbidden haplotype combinations, and (when phenotype data exist) phenotype association. Results combine into a per-candidate confidence ladder (weak → moderate → strong → very strong). WGS-only signals are correlative — controlled crosses are required for validation.

MANUSCRIPT PHRASING
Although direct validation of Bateson–Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibilities requires controlled crosses or fitness measurements, the present dataset allows candidate incompatibility regions to be screened by testing for segregation distortion, depletion of expected inversion genotype classes, ancestry-dependent karyotype frequencies, and long-range non-random associations between inversion arrangements and genomic backgrounds.
For linked inversions on the same chromosome, apparent distortion at one candidate should be tested conditionally on the other candidate or on the inferred long-range haplotype regime. If the distortion disappears after conditioning, the signal is better interpreted as haplotype-regime coupling than as independent underdominance, meiotic drive, or BDMI. Once a residual signal survives the regime control, the cross-type classifier separates meiotic drive (the parent transmits one arrangement too often) from underdominance (the heterozygote offspring class is depleted regardless of cross).

C. Meiotic drive vs underdominance (cross-type classifier)
Aggregates DEMO.triads by parent cross-type at the focal candidate. Drive candidate = AB parent under-transmits the same allele across crosses. Underdominance candidate = the AB offspring class is depleted regardless of cross direction. The classifier defers to Section B when the focal is regime-explained.

INTERPRETATION HIERARCHY
Before calling a candidate underdominance, meiotic drive, or BDMI, check these in order:
  1. Single-inversion distortion (Section A — focal HWE marginal).
  2. Conditional on nearby inversion (Section B — partner table).
  3. Conditional on same-chromosome haplotype regime (Section B — coupled / explained / residual verdict).
  4. Conditional on genome-wide ancestry (cross-page — BDMI Test D).
  5. Conditional on family / pedigree (cross-page — Mendelian tab cohort dyads / triads).
  6. Only then assign a mechanistic label (Section C — drive vs underdominance), and only after that go to BDMI / incompatibility (BDMI tab).
Meiotic crossover observables. From offspring genotypes alone you can estimate single-CO and double-CO frequencies and derive the coefficient of coincidence C and the interference I = 1 − C. The latent two-step model (precondition d followed by exchange x) is not identifiable from genotype data because the three observed quantities (r₁, r₂, r₁₂) underdetermine the four unknowns (a, b, d, x). Treat d and x as conceptual placeholders only — separate them only if you have an independent assay (DSBs, recombination nodules, SPO11 oligos, MLH1 foci).

Reduced single-CO / double-CO inside inversion spans is one of the standard expectations for paracentric / pericentric inversions in heterokaryotypes. The Regime inheritance tab tests whether linked inversions inherit as one block — low CO between two coupled inversions is consistent with that.
